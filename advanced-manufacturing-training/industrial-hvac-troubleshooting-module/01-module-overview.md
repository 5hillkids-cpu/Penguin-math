# Industrial HVAC Electrical and Control Troubleshooting

## Module Overview
- Topic area: Industrial HVAC electrical systems, control sequences, instrumentation, and mechanical troubleshooting
- Target audience: Maintenance technicians, facilities technicians, HVAC apprentices, and operations support staff
- Recommended duration: 5 hours instructor-led plus 45 minutes digital prework
- Delivery format: Blended technical training with digital intro module, instructor-led instruction, guided troubleshooting, and virtual lab
- Prerequisites: Electrical safety, multimeter basics, relay logic familiarity, and introductory HVAC component identification
- Required tools and materials: HVAC wiring schematic, control ladder, thermostat and sensor point list, multimeter, clamp meter, pressure readings, sequence-of-operation document, and HMI or BAS screenshots

## Safety Requirements
- Use lockout-tagout for electrical isolation before opening control panels.
- Verify zero energy on line and control voltage circuits before resistance checks.
- Treat rotating fans and belts as stored-energy hazards.
- Follow site policy for refrigerant-related tasks; this module focuses on controls and diagnostics, not refrigerant handling.

## Learning Objectives
By the end of the module, learners will be able to:

1. Explain the sequence of operation for an industrial air-handling or packaged HVAC unit.
2. Identify how safeties, sensors, relays, contactors, and control boards affect unit operation.
3. Interpret common HVAC no-start, short-cycling, and airflow-related faults using electrical and control evidence.
4. Diagnose a unit trip or failure by combining symptoms, voltage checks, sensor values, and sequence logic.
5. Verify corrective action and return the unit to safe operation.

## Assessment Strategy
- Embedded verbal checks during instruction
- Workbook tables and troubleshooting logs
- Instructor observation during fault-isolation exercises
- Virtual lab completion
- End-of-module knowledge check

## Module Scenario Theme
An industrial rooftop air handler fails to bring on the supply fan after a morning warm-up call. The BAS shows a demand signal, but the fan contactor never energizes. Learners must determine whether the issue is caused by a safety string opening, bad control transformer output, failed relay, incorrect sensor reading, or mechanical device condition.
