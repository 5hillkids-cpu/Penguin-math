# Conveyor System Electrical and PLC Troubleshooting

## Module Overview
- Topic area: Industrial electrical systems, instrumentation, PLC logic, and conveyor mechanical troubleshooting
- Target audience: Maintenance technicians, mechatronics apprentices, and industrial operations support staff
- Recommended duration: 4.5 hours instructor-led plus 60 minutes self-paced digital reinforcement
- Delivery format: Blended learning with Rise or Storyline prework, instructor-led lesson, guided troubleshooting, and virtual lab
- Prerequisites: Basic electrical safety, multimeter use, ladder diagram familiarity, and introductory PLC concepts
- Required tools and materials: Conveyor trainer or line schematic, digital multimeter, clamp meter, hand tools, lockout-tagout kit, PLC I/O list, fault logs, and HMI screenshots

## Safety Requirements
- Follow lockout-tagout before opening panels or adjusting mechanical components.
- Verify zero energy state on electrical and pneumatic sources.
- Keep guards in place unless a supervised troubleshooting step requires removal.
- Use proper PPE for energized testing when permitted by site policy.

## Learning Objectives
By the end of the module, learners will be able to:

1. Explain the sequence of operation for a basic motor-driven conveyor controlled by a PLC.
2. Trace how field devices, inputs, logic conditions, and outputs interact during conveyor start and stop commands.
3. Interpret common sensor, overload, VFD, and jam-related faults using electrical readings and PLC status indicators.
4. Diagnose a conveyor failure by combining operator symptoms, instrument readings, PLC logic clues, and mechanical observations.
5. Recommend and verify corrective actions that return the conveyor to normal operation.

## Module Scenario Theme
The learner supports a packaging line where Conveyor 3 intermittently refuses to start after a jam-clear event. The operator reports that the HMI shows Ready, but pressing Start does not energize the motor contactor. Learners must determine whether the issue is caused by a field input, control logic interlock, overload condition, drive permissive, or mechanical jam signal.
