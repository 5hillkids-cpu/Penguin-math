# Georgia Mechanical Facilitator Guide

## Module
Industrial Chiller Control and Diagnostics

## Delivery Snapshot
- Audience: HVAC controls technicians, maintenance technicians, utilities operators
- Recommended duration: 5.5 hours
- Delivery mode: Instructor-led classroom and scenario lab
- Training standard: Georgia Mechanical protected-equipment troubleshooting method

## Required Materials
- Chiller sequence-of-operation document
- Alarm history printouts
- Controller screenshots
- Sensor plausibility examples
- Learner workbook
- Slide deck or slide script

## Facilitation Goals
1. Teach learners to respect protection logic.
2. Build the habit of comparing controller truth to process truth.
3. Prevent repeated resets and unproven compressor blame.
4. Strengthen sensor plausibility reasoning.

## Timed Delivery Plan

### Segment 1: Protected Equipment Framing, 20 minutes
- Facilitator action: Open with a compressor lockout scenario.
- Talking point: A reset is not a diagnosis.
- Transition line: "We first need to understand what the controller must see before it will permit the compressor."

### Segment 2: Enable Sequence and Safeties, 40 minutes
- Facilitator action: Walk through demand, pump proof, flow proof, temperature logic, and compressor enable.
- Activity: Learners identify which conditions are process-based and which are control-based.
- Transition line: "Now that the logic is clear, we can compare it to the fault data."

### Segment 3: Data Sources and Alarm Interpretation, 40 minutes
- Facilitator action: Review alarm history, controller values, and independent field measurements.
- Talking point: Alarm text is a clue, not always the full diagnosis.
- Activity: Learners identify which data source is most trustworthy and why.
- Transition line: "The next step is deciding whether the controller is wrong or whether the controller is reacting to wrong input."

### Segment 4: Scenario Evidence Review, 45 minutes
- Facilitator action: Present the low leaving-water reading alongside the warmer handheld reading.
- Talking point: Conflicting measurements create a sensor plausibility question.
- Activity: Learners write what the controller believes and whether that belief is reasonable.
- Transition line: "Now we can move from alarm interpretation to root cause."

### Segment 5: Root Cause Logic, 35 minutes
- Facilitator action: Lead learners to the faulty temperature sensor diagnosis.
- Talking point: The controller behavior is correct given the false input.
- Activity: Learners draft a one-minute explanation for a supervisor.
- Transition line: "A protected machine is only ready for reset after valid input is restored."

### Segment 6: Corrective Action and Reset Discipline, 25 minutes
- Facilitator action: Model sensor verification, replacement or recalibration, and monitored restart.
- Talking point: Reset comes after evidence, not before it.
- Activity: Learners place corrective steps in order.
- Transition line: "You will now apply the same logic with less scaffolding."

### Segment 7: Workbook and Virtual Lab, 60 minutes
- Facilitator action: Move learners into scenario analysis and independent reasoning.
- Coaching prompt: "What does the controller know, and what do you know that the controller does not?"

### Segment 8: Debrief and Close, 15 minutes
- Facilitator action: Summarize the Georgia Mechanical standard for protected equipment.
- Close line: "The controller may be right, the sensor may be wrong, and the technician’s job is to prove which is true before any reset."

## Instructor Notes
- Stop learners from calling the compressor bad without supporting evidence.
- Keep the distinction clear between process condition and instrumentation condition.
- Reinforce that resets without validation are poor troubleshooting behavior.

## Success Indicators
- Learners identify the conflicting data points.
- Learners explain why the lockout logic is still reasonable.
- Learners justify sensor verification before reset.
